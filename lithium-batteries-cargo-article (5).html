The Challenges of Lithium-Ion Batteries as Cargo at Sea

By Michael Oder ‚Ä¢ Maritime Emergency Response

Lithium-ion batteries power everything from smartphones and laptops to electric vehicles (EVs) and energy storage systems. Their high energy density and versatility have made them indispensable in the modern economy‚Äîbut these same qualities present significant risks when shipped in bulk on ocean going vessels. As maritime transport accounts for roughly 90% of global trade, ensuring the safe carriage of such batteries has become a top priority for regulators, carriers, and insurers alike.

Understanding Lithium-Ion Battery Technology

Lithium-ion batteries store and release energy by moving lithium ions between two electrodes through a liquid electrolyte.

  • Anode (negative): usually graphite, where lithium ions are stored when the battery is charged.
  • Cathode (positive): a lithium-metal oxide that receives lithium ions during discharge.
  • Electrolyte & separator: let lithium ions move between electrodes while keeping them electrically isolated.

How it works:

  • Charging: an external power source pushes lithium ions from the cathode to the anode; electrons flow through the charger.
  • Discharging: lithium ions move back to the cathode; electrons flow through your device, providing power.

It's rechargeable, energy-dense, and efficient‚Äîwhich is why it's used in phones, laptops, EVs, and many industrial applications.

Inherent Safety Risks

The greatest challenge with Li-ion batteries stems from their chemistry and physical characteristics:

  • Thermal runaway: A defect, physical damage, overcharging, or internal short circuit can trigger a chain reaction inside a battery cell, rapidly increasing its temperature and releasing flammable gases. Once initiated, this process can be extremely difficult to stop and may lead to fires or explosions.
  • Intense fires: Fires involving Li-ion batteries burn at very high temperatures and can reignite even after initial suppression. When such fires occur within the dense confines of a container ship or car deck, they are hard to detect and suppress effectively.
  • Toxic gases: Burning batteries release hazardous gases that pose risks to crew safety and can damage ship infrastructure and cargo.

These hazards are especially acute in enclosed spaces like container ship cargo holds and on roll-on/roll-off (Ro-Ro) car carriers where electric vehicles‚Äîeach with substantial battery capacity‚Äîare shipped in large numbers.

Detection and Emergency Response Challenges

Responding to a Li-ion battery fire at sea is uniquely difficult:

  • Delayed detection: Batteries in sealed containers or tightly packed vehicle decks can build up heat, undetected, for long periods.
  • Fire suppression limitations: Most shipboard firefighting systems were not designed for high-temperature chemical fires. Standard CO‚ÇÇ or water-based systems may not stop thermal runaway effectively, and using seawater can cause corrosion and electrical hazards.
  • Remote operations: Vessels at sea are far from shore-based fire response resources, meaning onboard crews must manage emergencies with limited support‚Äîoften with little time to react.

This combination of late detection and limited response capability makes Li-ion battery fires particularly formidable and hazardous.

Regulatory and Compliance Issues

Maritime regulations attempt to mitigate risks, but challenges remain:

  • IMDG classification: Lithium batteries are classified as Class 9 hazardous materials under the International Maritime Dangerous Goods (IMDG) Code, requiring specific packaging, labeling, and documentation.
  • Misdeclaration problems: A major cause of cargo fires has been improper or incomplete declaration of battery shipments, often to avoid regulatory costs or paperwork. Such misdeclarations hinder proper stowage and emergency planning.

Well-Documented Maritime Incidents

COSCO Pacific (January 2020)

A major container fire aboard the containership COSCO Pacific was traced to a mis declared shipment of lithium-ion batteries. The Bill of Lading described the containers as "spare parts and accessories," not hazardous goods, leading to incorrect stowage and delayed hazard awareness. The fire broke out in three containers loaded in Nansha (China) and bound for Nhava Sheva (India), forcing the vessel to divert to Colombo, Sri Lanka, for inspection.

Mis declared Scrap Battery Container Fire ‚Äî San Pedro Bay (March 2022)

In the Port of Los Angeles-Long Beach area, a shipping container loaded with scrapped lithium-ion batteries ‚Äî erroneously declared as "synthetic resins" ‚Äî caught fire while awaiting loading on a vessel. This fire required a large fire response, and the US Coast Guard subsequently held all containers from the same shipper pending verification of correct declarations ‚Äî a clear example of how misdeclaration compounds port and vessel risk.

Mis declared Battery Fires Cited by USCG (August 2021)

The U.S. Coast Guard's safety alerts highlighted additional incidents where containers carrying used lithium batteries ‚Äî mis declared as "computer parts" ‚Äî caught fire. Although some fires occurred before loading aboard ships, the intensity and hazard of these fires underscored how such cargo could produce catastrophic fires at sea if aboard a containership.

  • Regulatory gaps: Some exemptions in the IMDG Code (e.g., Special Provision 188 for small batteries) can inadvertently encourage misdeclaration. Industry groups have called for tighter classification and segregation requirements.

These regulatory complexities add significant administrative burden for shippers and carriers while also highlighting the need for clearer, more effective global standards.

DDR vs End of Life Li Batteries

Damaged, Defective or Recalled (DDR) Li Batteries are not allowed to be shipped via ocean going vessels.

Core Regulation: IMDG Code

IMDG Code ‚Äì Special Provision 376 (SP 376)

Lithium batteries that are damaged or defective, or that have failed safety tests, are forbidden for transport by sea. This applies to:

  • Lithium-ion batteries (UN 3480 / UN 3481)
  • Lithium metal batteries (UN 3090 / UN 3091)

Examples of "damaged or defective" under SP 376 include:

  • Batteries involved in fire or thermal events
  • Swollen, leaking, crushed, or punctured batteries
  • Batteries with internal short-circuit risk
  • Batteries showing abnormal heat generation
  • Batteries recalled by the manufacturer for safety reasons

The Only Possible Exception (Rare and Controlled)

Transport may be permitted only if all of the following apply:

  • Shipment is for disposal, recycling, or analysis
  • Approved by the competent authority of the country of origin and the flag state / port state (as applicable)
  • Batteries are packed in UN-approved, fire-mitigating packaging
  • Additional controls are applied (often including temperature control, isolation, and voyage restrictions)

‚ö†Ô∏è Even under this exception:

  • Many carriers flatly refuse these shipments
  • Many ports will not accept them
  • Class societies may prohibit them aboard classed vessels

Additional IMDG Provisions That Reinforce the Ban

Special Provision 377: Prohibits lithium batteries identified as being for disposal or recycling unless they meet SP 376 requirements. Prevents shippers from disguising DDR batteries as "waste electronics."

Special Provision 230: Requires lithium batteries to meet UN 38.3 testing. DDR batteries typically fail or cannot demonstrate compliance.

Special Provision 310: Clarifies documentation and classification requirements. Reinforces that non-compliant batteries are forbidden.

Special Provision 388 (Newer IMDG Editions): Tightens controls on batteries with manufacturing defects. Addresses latent defects that may not show visible damage.

Why the Maritime Rules Are So Strict

The IMO and IMDG Code explicitly recognize that:

  • Lithium battery fires cannot be reliably extinguished at sea
  • Thermal runaway can propagate container-to-container
  • DDR batteries have a much higher probability of spontaneous ignition
  • A single DDR battery shipment can result in total vessel loss

This is why the IMDG Code treats DDR lithium batteries differently than intact, compliant batteries.

Carrier and Port Enforcement (Beyond the IMDG Code)

Even where the IMDG Code allows a narrow exception:

  • Most major ocean carriers prohibit DDR lithium batteries entirely
  • Ports may deny berth, anchorage, or discharge
  • Misdeclared DDR batteries may trigger cargo seizure, heavy fines, criminal liability, and blacklisting of shippers

Economic and Supply Chain Impacts

The challenges go beyond safety, affecting global trade flows:

  • Carrier reluctance: Shipping lines may refuse lithium battery cargo due to risk exposure, leading to delays or higher freight costs.
  • Supply chain disruption: Complex approval processes and fear of liability can slow the movement of goods, particularly as demand for batteries and EVs grows.

Insurers have also noted increasing costs associated with cargo fires, pushing the industry to demand stricter compliance and new safety solutions.

Looking Forward: Industry Responses

To address these multifaceted challenges, stakeholders are pursuing several strategies:

  • Enhanced training: Educating crews on Li-ion fire behavior and response tactics is critical to reducing damage and injury.
  • Improved technology: Advanced fire detection and battery-specific suppression systems are under development to better handle thermal runaway.
  • Regulatory reform: Industry bodies and authorities are evaluating revisions to the IMDG Code and SOLAS (Safety of Life at Sea) regulations to improve stowage rules, classification systems, and monitoring protocols.
  • AI and risk screening: The industry has begun using artificial intelligence to detect patterns of mis declared dangerous goods, aiming to intercept high-risk cargo before it reaches a ship.

Conclusion

Transporting lithium-ion batteries by sea is essential to supporting today's energy transition and digital economy‚Äîbut it comes with serious challenges. The combination of chemical volatility, detection difficulties, firefighting limitations, regulatory complexities, and economic impacts presents a formidable risk profile for carriers and crews. Addressing these issues requires coordinated efforts across regulation, technology, training, and supply chain practices to make maritime battery transport safer and more reliable in the years ahead.
